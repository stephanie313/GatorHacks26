How the six metrics connect

PulseMap treats these six indicators as a system—not isolated statistics. Poverty drives food insecurity; food stress raises diabetes, obesity, and depression risk; and being uninsured makes every chronic condition harder to manage. K-Means clustering surfaces where these burdens stack up, and the Action Guide maps each concern to steps from your source library.

  • Food-insecure adults face higher chronic-disease risk, including diabetes. Pair nutrition access with screening and lifestyle prevention steps.
  • Food insecurity often means lower diet quality—not just less food—which can worsen obesity risk. Address affordability and nutrition support together.
  • Food stress and mental health burdens frequently overlap in the same communities. Coordinate food programs with depression care and follow-up.
  • Households often choose between food, housing, and healthcare. SNAP/WIC and coverage enrollment can relieve the same budget pressure.
  • Coverage gaps make prediabetes screening and prevention harder to reach. Prioritize enrollment help alongside community diabetes-prevention programs.
  • Without insurance, therapy and follow-up care are harder to sustain. Pair coverage navigation with evidence-based mental health treatment access.

🫁 Asthma

Structured asthma pathways combine symptom tracking, trigger reduction, and a written action plan so attacks are caught early.

  1. Track symptoms and rescue inhaler use; seek care if rescue use increases.
  2. Work with your clinician on trigger awareness (smoke, pollution, allergens).
  3. Follow a written asthma action plan and keep controller meds on schedule.
  4. Ask about home peak flow monitoring if your care team recommends it.

Source: Chan et al., eClinicalMedicine 2025 (digital asthma journey)

🩸 Diabetes

Lifestyle programs can prevent or delay type 2 diabetes. Screening, weight loss, activity, and diet changes work in clinics and communities.

  1. Ask about diabetes screening (A1C, fasting glucose, or glucose tolerance) if you are overweight or have risk factors—many prediabetes cases go undiagnosed.
  2. If at risk, join a lifestyle program targeting about 7% weight loss and at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.
  3. Improve diet quality: more fiber and whole grains, less saturated fat; reduce sugar-sweetened drinks.
  4. Ask for brief counseling, group diabetes-prevention sessions, or a referral to a local program (clinic, YMCA, or telehealth).

Source: Galaviz et al., American Journal of Lifestyle Medicine 2018

🛡️ Uninsured

Coverage gaps drive inequitable access, avoidable harm, and poorly managed chronic conditions. State and community programs can expand affordable options.

  1. Check eligibility for Medicaid, CHIP, or state programs that cover low-income children—and whether your state covers low-income adults.
  2. If you work without employer insurance, ask about state insurance pools that subsidize premiums for small businesses and the self-employed.
  3. Prioritize a regular primary care source; being uninsured is linked to missed preventive care and poorly managed chronic conditions.
  4. Connect with local enrollment help to compare individual or marketplace coverage and reduce gaps that force emergency-only care.

Source: Davis, BMJ 2007 (Uninsured in America: problems and possible solutions)

⚖️ Obesity

Modest, sustained changes to diet and physical activity support healthy weight loss and lower cardiometabolic risk.

  1. Combine modest diet changes with regular walking or activity most days.
  2. Reduce sugary drinks and high-fat processed foods; add fruits and vegetables.
  3. Use smaller portions and limit long stretches of sitting or screen time.
  4. Ask your clinic about structured nutrition or weight-support programs nearby.

Source: Lean et al., BMJ 2006 (ABC of Obesity)

🧠 Depression

Evidence-based psychotherapy and sustained follow-up toward remission reduce long-term burden; crisis support is available when needed.

  1. Ask about evidence-based therapy such as CBT or interpersonal therapy (IPT).
  2. Keep follow-up visits until symptoms clearly improve—treat toward remission.
  3. Tell your doctor if treatment is not helping after about six weeks.
  4. Be open about alcohol or drug use; call or text 988 if you are in crisis.

🍎 Food Insecurity

Poverty is the primary driver—not grocery distance alone. When food costs compete with rent, energy, and healthcare, households face trade-offs that raise risk for diabetes, obesity, and depression.

Connects with: Diabetes, Obesity, Depression, Uninsured

  1. Apply for SNAP and ask about WIC or school meal programs if affording quality food is difficult.
  2. Connect with local food banks, co-ops, or mutual aid networks—not only when stores are far away.
  3. When food, rent, and medical costs compete, combine nutrition help with coverage navigation in the same plan.
  4. Volunteer, donate, or advocate locally for stronger SNAP benefits and universal school lunch access.

USDA Food & Nutrition Service (SNAP, WIC, school meals)

Source: PovertyUSA.org, "What Causes Food Insecurity and What are Solutions to It?"
